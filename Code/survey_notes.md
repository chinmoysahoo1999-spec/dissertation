## 1. MIND — Su et al. 2024 (the methodology the student is implementing)

> File: `hallucination_detection using unsupervised method.pdf`. The earlier project memo (STATUS.md) said the MIND PDF wasn't present; that turns out to be wrong — this file *is* MIND. There is no separate "MIND" PDF in addition.

- **Full title / authors.** "Unsupervised Real-Time Hallucination Detection based on the Internal States of Large Language Models." Weihang Su, Changyue Wang, Qingyao Ai, Yiran Hu, Zhijing Wu, Yujia Zhou, Yiqun Liu (Tsinghua / BIT / Renmin).
- **Venue / year.** Findings of ACL 2024 (pp. 14379–14391).
- **Core method (3–5 sentences).** MIND is an unsupervised framework for *real-time* hallucination detection from internal states. (1) **Pseudo-label generation:** Wikipedia (WikiText-103) articles are truncated at a random named entity occurring after the first sentence; the LLM continues writing from the truncation point; the continuation is compared against the original article — if the first entity the LLM emits matches the gold entity it is non-hallucination (label 0), otherwise hallucination (label 1). (2) **Hidden-state feature:** the contextualised embedding of the last token at the last Transformer layer is selected as input to an MLP classifier (4-layer, ReLU, 256→128→64→2, dropout 0.2, LR 5e-4, batch 32). (3) At inference, the trained MLP is fed the LLM's hidden state token-by-token during generation, producing a per-token / per-sentence hallucination probability with negligible overhead (~3% of generation time).
- **Key technique.** Internal-state probing + automatic entity-substitution self-supervision over Wikipedia continuations. No human labels.
- **Datasets.** The authors introduce **HELM** (Hallucination detection Evaluation for multiple LLMs): 3582 sentences from 1224 passages, generated by 6 LLMs (Falcon-40B, GPT-J-6B, LLaMA-2-Base-7B, LLaMA-2-Chat-7B, LLaMA-2-Chat-13B, OPT-6.7B), human-annotated factuality at sentence and passage level. Pseudo-training data is auto-generated from WikiText-103.
- **Models.** Six LLMs above are evaluated. The probe was trained primarily on LLaMA-2-13B-Chat data (5k samples) and tested across models with model-specific re-training (their ablation Table 4 shows customised training data is essential).
- **Key result / claim.** MIND outperforms PP/PE, all four SelfCheckGPT variants, SAPLMA, EUBHD, and GPT-4-as-judge on HELM at both sentence and passage levels. E.g. sentence-level AUC on LLaMA-2-Chat-7B = 0.6755, passage-level = 0.8547 (best in their table). MIND inference time per response = 0.05s vs. SelfCheckGPT-NLI 15.3s (~300× faster). Customised per-LLM training data is critical (Table 4); plain SAPLMA-style "borrowed" data hurts.
- **How MIND relates to itself.** It *is* the method being implemented. Important details for the student's reproduction:
  - The hidden-state extraction uses the *last-token, last-layer* embedding by default — confirmed best by their ablation (Acc 0.7123) vs. first-token (0.6529), all-layers-avg (0.7054), or mixed views (0.7191).
  - 4-layer MLP, ReLU, dropout 0.2, hidden sizes 256/128/64/2. The student's `src/classifier.py` uses parameterised hidden widths via `config.HIDDEN_LAYERS` — match these defaults.
  - Pseudo-label heuristic: an entity (NER via spaCy) is "matched" if the LLM's continuation *starts with* the gold entity. Otherwise hallucination. The student's `src/entities.py` and `src/tokens.py` mirror this.
  - 5k training samples sufficed for LLaMA-2-13B-Chat; 4096 was the sweet spot in Table 5 (0.7237 acc).
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Direct. The student's pipeline (4-bit Llama-3.2-1B, entity-substitution on Wikipedia, MLP on `get_hd` embeddings) is a faithful re-implementation. The chapter should cite this as the primary methodological reference and note that the student extends MIND beyond HELM/Wikipedia to multi-task QA / summ / dialog benchmarks.

## 2. HaloScope — Du, Xiao, Li, NeurIPS 2024

- **Full title / authors.** "HaloScope: Harnessing Unlabeled LLM Generations for Hallucination Detection." Xuefeng Du, Chaowei Xiao, Yixuan Li (UW-Madison).
- **Venue / year.** NeurIPS 2024.
- **Core method.** Two-stage learning from *unlabelled* LLM outputs. (1) **Membership estimation via latent subspace:** collect embeddings F ∈ R^{N×d} from unlabeled LLM generations, center, do SVD F = UΣV⊤; project each sample onto top-k singular vectors and score ζ_i = (1/k)Σ σ_j ⟨f_i, v_j⟩². Truthful and hallucinated samples are assumed to have different ζ magnitudes, with truthful concentrated near the mean and hallucinated farther out. (2) **Truthfulness classifier:** a 2-layer MLP (hidden 1024, ReLU) trained with a sigmoid-loss surrogate to separate samples scored above the threshold (hallucinated) from below (truthful). At test time the classifier outputs S(x).
- **Key technique.** SVD on activations + binary classifier from pseudo-labels.
- **Datasets.** TruthfulQA (817 QA), TriviaQA (rc.nocontext, 9960), CoQA (dev 7983), TydiQA-GP English (3696). 25% held out for test, 100 for validation.
- **Models.** LLaMA-2-chat 7B & 13B, OPT 6.7B & 13B. Hidden states are extracted from the prompt+answer last token. For MHA they ablate three locations (block output `f`, `Attn(f)`, `Q Attn(f)`); LLaMA prefers block output; OPT prefers feedforward output.
- **Key result.** AUROC on TruthfulQA / TriviaQA / CoQA / TydiQA-GP for LLaMA-2-7B-chat: **78.64 / 77.40 / 76.42 / 94.04** — outperforming SelfCheckGPT, Semantic Entropy, EigenScore, Perplexity, LN-entropy, Verbalize, Self-evaluation, CCS, CCS*. They beat the next best (CCS*) by **+10.69%** on TruthfulQA. Approach is single-sampling (no multiple generations needed).
- **How it relates to MIND.** Same family — internal-state probing trained without human labels. Key differences:
  1. HaloScope uses **SVD-based subspace** as the *labelling function*; MIND uses an **entity-substitution heuristic** on Wikipedia continuations.
  2. HaloScope works on prompted QA pairs (last-token of question+answer); MIND works on free continuations from Wikipedia.
  3. HaloScope is layer-sensitive (best at middle layers ~8–14 on LLaMA-2-7B-chat); MIND uses last layer.
  4. Both train a small MLP on top.
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Direct comparison target. The student should report HaloScope numbers on the same multi-task QA setup. The Dasgupta thesis already reports HaloScope as a baseline (see Section 14 below); the student's HalluShift-style extension would *also* be an interesting baseline to consider.

## 3. SelfCheckGPT — Manakul, Liusie, Gales 2023

- **Full title / authors.** "SelfCheckGPT: Zero-Resource Black-Box Hallucination Detection for Generative Large Language Models." Potsawee Manakul, Adian Liusie, Mark J. F. Gales (Cambridge).
- **Venue / year.** EMNLP 2023 (arXiv 2303.08896).
- **Core method.** Black-box, zero-resource sampling-based detection. Given a main response R and N stochastic samples {S^1…S^N} from the same prompt (temperature 1.0, N=20), measure information consistency between R and the samples. If a sentence in R is "supported" across samples it is likely factual; if it diverges it is likely hallucinated. Five score variants implementing the consistency check: **BERTScore**, **MQAG (question answering)**, **n-gram** (avg or max negative log-prob from a small n-gram LM fit on samples), **NLI** (DeBERTa-v3-large fine-tuned on MNLI; contradiction score), **Prompt** (LLM judge prompted "Is the sentence supported by the context? Yes/No"). Final score is averaged over samples to give an inconsistency score per sentence; passage scores average sentence scores.
- **Key technique.** Sampling + consistency (lexical / semantic / NLI / LLM-judge variants).
- **Datasets.** WikiBio GPT-3 dataset: 238 GPT-3 (text-davinci-003) generated Wikipedia-style passages about individuals (1908 sentences), manually annotated as Major Inaccurate / Minor Inaccurate / Accurate.
- **Models.** GPT-3 (text-davinci-003) for generation. LLaMA used as a proxy LLM baseline (grey-box). ChatGPT (gpt-3.5-turbo) and GPT-3 used as judges for the Prompt variant.
- **Key result.** Sentence-level non-factual AUC-PR: SelfCheck-Prompt **93.42** > NLI 92.50 > Unigram(max) 85.63 > QA 84.26 > BERTScore 81.96 > GPT-3 Max(-logp) 87.51. Passage Pearson correlation with human: Prompt 78.32 > NLI 74.14. SelfCheckGPT was the first work to consider general LLM hallucination detection.
- **How it relates to MIND.** Both target free-form generation. SelfCheckGPT is **black-box** (no internal states, no token logits required) and **sampling-based** (N≈20 samples) — MIND requires internal states and a labelled corpus but is single-pass and ~300× faster at inference. MIND's HELM benchmark explicitly compares against SCG variants and beats them at sentence-level.
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Must-have baseline. Provides four naturally implementable variants (BERTScore, n-gram, NLI, Prompt) that don't require model internals. The chapter's "Sampling-based" section is centred on this paper.

## 4. Azaria & Mitchell 2023 — SAPLMA (the precursor to MIND)

- **Full title / authors.** "The Internal State of an LLM Knows When It's Lying." Amos Azaria (Ariel University) and Tom Mitchell (CMU).
- **Venue / year.** arXiv 2304.13734 (Oct 2023). EMNLP 2023 findings.
- **Core method.** SAPLMA = Statement Accuracy Prediction based on Language Model Activations. A 3-hidden-layer feedforward classifier (sigmoid output, Adam) receives the hidden-state activations of an LLM as it *reads* a true/false statement. The classifier predicts P(true | hidden). Crucially, training is "leave-one-topic-out": train on 5 topics, test on the 6th, so the classifier must extract a topic-agnostic truth signal rather than topic-correlated patterns.
- **Key technique.** Supervised hidden-state probing on hand-crafted true/false statements.
- **Datasets.** Authors release a 6,084-sentence "True-False" dataset: Cities, Inventions, Chemical Elements, Animals, Companies, Scientific Facts. False statements are produced by swapping factual attributes (e.g. "atomic number of Hydrogen is 34") — directly parallel to MIND's entity substitution. They also test on LLM-generated sentences (OPT-6.7B → 245 statements labelled by 2 judges).
- **Models.** OPT-6.7B (Zhang et al. 2022) and LLaMA-2-7B. They probe last layer, 28th (12th-before-last), and middle layer. OPT: best on 20th layer (86.4%); LLaMA-2-7B: middle layer is best.
- **Key result.** Average accuracy: 60–80% per topic for OPT-6.7B, 70–90% for LLaMA-2-7B. Few-shot prompting baseline ≤56%. The "It is true that X" probability baseline ≤59%. SAPLMA outperforms sentence-probability baselines by ~20% on the LLM-generated test set.
- **How it relates to MIND.** SAPLMA is MIND's direct precursor and the conceptual seed for hidden-state probing of truthfulness. Key differences:
  1. **Labels:** SAPLMA needs hand-crafted true/false sentences (6 topics); MIND auto-generates labels from Wikipedia by entity substitution — *no human annotation*.
  2. **Mode:** SAPLMA primarily evaluates *reading* mode (model reads the sentence); MIND operates during *generation*.
  3. **Architecture / layer:** SAPLMA uses 3-layer MLP and probes per-layer; MIND uses 4-layer MLP and last layer last token by default.
  4. **Outcome:** MIND outperforms SAPLMA on HELM because SAPLMA's training data (hand-crafted) doesn't match the LLM's own generation distribution (Table 4 in MIND confirms model-specific data matters).
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Foundational citation. Establishes the empirical claim that hidden states encode truthfulness. Two related files (`The internal state of LLM know when it laying(2023).pdf` and `Study_internal state of LLM Know when it is laying paper.pdf`) both refer to this paper; the latter is a study-notes summary.

## 5. Kadavath et al. 2022 — "Language Models (Mostly) Know What They Know"

- **Full title / authors.** "Language Models (Mostly) Know What They Know." Saurav Kadavath, Tom Conerly, Amanda Askell, et al. (Anthropic), led by Jared Kaplan.
- **Venue / year.** arXiv 2207.05221 (Jul–Nov 2022).
- **Core method.** Two calibration probes. (1) **P(True):** the model first proposes an answer, then is prompted to self-evaluate whether its proposed answer is true; the probability assigned to "True" is the calibration signal. Multiple sample comparison improves accuracy. (2) **P(IK):** a separately trained value head on top of the LLM that predicts, from the question alone, the probability that the model will sample a correct answer ("I Know"). Trained from many sampled rollouts. They study calibration, generalisation across tasks, and the effect of including source materials / hints in the prompt.
- **Key technique.** Self-evaluation prompting + a trained-on-internals probability head ("P(IK)").
- **Datasets.** BIG-Bench (multiple choice), MMLU, TruthfulQA, LogiQA, QuALITY (multiple choice / true-false); TriviaQA, Lambada, Codex HumanEval, GSM8k, arithmetic, function synthesis (generative).
- **Models.** Anthropic's 800M – 52B parameter Transformer family.
- **Key result.** Large LMs are well-calibrated on multiple-choice and true/false in the right format. P(True) self-evaluation shows non-trivial accuracy gains for filtering correct samples (Figure 1 of paper). P(IK) generalises *partially* across tasks (e.g. trained on TriviaQA, tested on arithmetic) but is poorly calibrated across distribution shifts; P(IK) appropriately rises with relevant source materials and correct hints.
- **How it relates to MIND.** Conceptually adjacent — both ask "does the model know what it knows?" — but operationally different. Kadavath uses *self-evaluation prompts* and trained value heads on the LLM itself; MIND trains an *external* probe on hidden states with auto-generated labels. SelfCheckGPT's "Prompt" variant directly descends from this paper's P(True) idea.
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Cite as the canonical calibration / self-knowledge reference. The student's MIND probe is a *more direct* readout of internal belief than P(True) — useful to frame in the related work section.

## 6. Ji et al. 2022/2024 — "Survey of Hallucination in Natural Language Generation"

> File: `Survey of hallucination in natural language generation.pdf`, also `2202.03629.pdf` (arXiv ID), also `latest survey on hallucination 6k + citation.pdf` — **all three are the same paper at different versions** (v6 Feb 2024 and v7 Jul 2024; the first two are versions of the same arXiv submission). The "6k citations" label is consistent with this paper, which has the highest citation count among hallucination surveys.

- **Full title / authors.** "Survey of Hallucination in Natural Language Generation." Ziwei Ji, Nayeon Lee, Rita Frieske, Tiezheng Yu, Dan Su, Yan Xu, Etsuko Ishii, Yejin Bang, Delong Chen, Wenliang Dai, Ho Shu Chan, Andrea Madotto, Pascale Fung (CAiRE, HKUST).
- **Venue / year.** ACM Computing Surveys, vol. 55 (12), 2023; later versions through Jul 2024 add LLM Section 13.
- **Core method (survey).** Comprehensive taxonomy of hallucination across NLG. Defines **intrinsic** (output contradicts source) vs. **extrinsic** (output unverifiable from source) hallucination. Categorises contributors (data noise, training/inference: exposure bias, parametric knowledge bias). Reviews **metrics**: Statistical (PARENT, knowledge-precision/F1), Model-based (IE-based, QA-based, NLI-based, faithfulness classifiers, LM-based), and Human Evaluation. Reviews **mitigation**: data-related (cleaning, augmentation, knowledge grounding) and modelling/inference (CoT, RAG, post-editing, multitask training, decoding). Then chapter-by-chapter task surveys: Summarisation, Dialogue, GQA, Data-to-Text, MT, Vision-Language, and (added 2024) LLMs.
- **Key datasets/benchmarks discussed.** WikiBio GPT-3 (SelfCheckGPT), HaluEval, HaDes, TruthfulQA, FactualityPrompt, FActScore, FEVER (for fact verification adjacent), MultiNLI, XSum, QAGS, FRANK, FaithDial, BEGIN, DialFact, Wizard-of-Wikipedia.
- **Models discussed.** All major LLMs and pre-LLM NLG models.
- **Key result / claim.** This is *the* canonical hallucination survey. Provides the standard taxonomy used throughout the literature. The 2024 LLM section adds discussion of HaluEval, FActScore, atomized factuality, GPT-4-as-judge, and "snowballing" hallucinations.
- **How it relates to MIND.** Reference / background. The Ji survey predates MIND but defines the vocabulary (intrinsic / extrinsic / faithfulness vs. factuality) and identifies open problems that MIND addresses (efficiency, model-specificity, reference-free detection).
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Background / framing. The student should cite Ji et al. for the categorisation in their definition section and for the taxonomy of evaluation metrics.

## 7. FEVER 2024 — Sevgili, Nikishina, Yimam, Semmann, Biemann (UHH at AVeriTeC)

> Files: `2024.fever-1.5.pdf` and `2024.fever-1.5-summary.pdf`. The summary file (by Chinmoy Sahoo, October 2025) is a plain-language summary of the main paper — the student themselves wrote the summary file.

- **Full title / authors.** "UHH at AVeriTeC: RAG for Fact-Checking with Real-World Claims." Özge Sevgili, Irina Nikishina, Seid Muhie Yimam, Martin Semmann, Chris Biemann (Universität Hamburg).
- **Venue / year.** Proceedings of the Seventh Fact Extraction and VERification Workshop (FEVER), ACL 2024, pages 55–63.
- **Core method.** RAG pipeline for the AVeriTeC fact-checking shared task. (1) Rank evidences with BM25, take top 10k. (2) Either **Retrieve-Question** (retrieve top-10 by vector similarity, generate questions for them, rerank by question-answer similarity) or **Question-Retrieve** (generate 3 sub-questions per claim, retrieve single best evidence per question). (3) Prompt an LLM to predict one of {Supported, Refuted, Not Enough Evidence, Conflicting/Cherry-Picking}, either over all evidences or per-evidence.
- **Key technique.** RAG with two-stage retrieval; not internal-state-based.
- **Datasets.** AVeriTeC shared task: 3068 train / 500 dev / 2215 test real-world claims from the Web.
- **Models.** Mixtral-style LLM (via Pinecone-style prompts); BM25 retriever; embedding model for vector similarity.
- **Key result.** Veracity@25 = **0.45** (vs. baseline 0.11) — 6th of 23 systems.
- **How it relates to MIND.** Adjacent / different family. Fact verification with retrieved evidence is the *RAG / external knowledge* family — orthogonal to hidden-state probing. Useful to position the survey: many hallucination "detection" papers in industry actually do retrieval-based fact verification.
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Adjacent — useful in the "Retrieval-augmented baselines" / "task-adjacent literature" sub-section, and also worth mentioning that the student's prior work is on this paper (the summary is authored by Chinmoy Sahoo). The chapter can use it as a contrast: MIND detects hallucination *without* retrieval; AVeriTeC-style systems verify *with* retrieval.

## 8. Lexical n-gram Metrics — Notes file

- **File.** `Notes On Lexical (n-gram) Metrics For Hallucination Detection.pdf`. This is a 4-page primer / notes document, not a peer-reviewed paper. No author/venue listed.
- **Core content.** Defines unigrams/bigrams/trigrams; reviews ROUGE / BLEU / METEOR; gives worked examples of high lexical overlap that still hallucinates ("Paris is the capital of Germany" has high n-gram overlap with "Paris is the capital of France" but is factually wrong); discusses PARENT as a partial fix that also compares to source data. Conclusion: lexical metrics are necessary baselines but cannot detect semantic contradictions or entity-attribute mis-binding.
- **Relevance to the student's project.** Useful for the chapter section on "Why we need internal-state-based methods." Frames the inadequacy of ROUGE/BLEU/METEOR for hallucination and motivates semantic / probing-based alternatives. Cite as a pedagogical reference rather than a primary source.

## 9. Sharanya Dasgupta — M.Tech thesis (ISI Kolkata, June 2025)

> File: `M.tech _Sharanya_Dasgupta_CS2320.pdf`. This is the most directly relevant prior thesis — same supervisor environment (ISI Kolkata, Prof. Swagatam Das), one year ahead. The student should treat this as the closest predecessor work.

- **Full title / authors.** "From Vigilance to Veracity: Hallucination Detection, Mitigation, and Safety Enhancement in Large Language Models." Sharanya Dasgupta (CS2320), supervised by Dr. Swagatam Das, Indian Statistical Institute, Kolkata. June 2025.
- **Two contributions.**
  1. **HalluShift** — a hallucination detection framework that fuses internal-state distribution shifts and token-probability features. Removes the manual layer-selection of prior work (Azaria & Mitchell, HaloScope) via automated range-wise feature selection across all LLM layers, then applies a series of statistical transformations.
  2. **ARREST** — a mitigation/safety framework. Locates the layer of maximum "concept misalignment" via a learned probe, then attaches an external GAN-style adversarial mitigator that performs hidden-state correction at inference. Two variants: Base Adversarial (mapping misaligned to aligned distributions via answer-injected prompts / RLHF-aligned activations) and Contrastive Adversarial (triplet loss with safety / jailbreak prompt pairs). Trains only ~33M parameters (~0.4% of original LLM).
- **HalluShift method details (most relevant to student).**
  - Input features: Wasserstein distance and cosine similarity between hidden states & attention features (capturing internal state distribution shift across layers), plus token-level probabilistic features (mtp, Mps, Mg).
  - Architecture: 2-layer MLP based on metric learning; features projected to a lower-dim latent, fused, and mapped to one output node (score in [0,1]).
  - Training: Weighted Adam, LR 1e-4 with adaptive scheduler, batch size 16, early stopping.
  - Compute: single RTX 3090 (24GB).
  - Generation config: greedy decoding, max output length 64 tokens — matching HaloScope.
- **Datasets.** TruthfulQA, TriviaQA, CoQA, TydiQA-GP (English), HaluEval-QA, HaluEval-Dialogue, HaluEval-Summarization. Standard sizes: CoQA 7983, TruthfulQA 817, TydiQA-GP 3696, TriviaQA 9960; HaluEval triple = 30k (10k per task).
- **Models.** LLaMA-2-7B, LLaMA-3.1-8B, OPT-6.7B, Vicuna-7B, Qwen2.5-7B (HalluShift); ARREST uses additional safety / aligned variants.
- **Key results (HalluShift).** AUROC % on (TruthfulQA / TriviaQA / CoQA / TydiQA-GP):
  - OPT-6.7B: **89.91 / 86.95 / 90.61 / 85.11** (HalluShift) vs. 73.17 / 72.36 / 77.64 / 80.98 (HaloScope) — gains of +16.74 / +14.59 / +12.97 / +4.13.
  - LLaMA-2-7B: **89.93 / 89.03 / 87.60 / 87.61** vs. 78.64 / 77.40 / 76.42 / 94.04 (HaloScope) — gains on first 3 tasks; HaloScope wins TydiQA-GP.
  - LLaMA-3.1-8B: 92.97 / 99.23 / 90.38 / 87.70.
  - Vicuna-7B: 80.37 / 89.18 / 79.43 / 73.37.
  - Qwen2.5-7B: 84.82 / 86.79 / 89.21 / 87.43.
  - On HaluEval: HalluShift vs. HalluDetect (Acc/F1/PRAUC):
    - Dialogue: **0.88/0.83/0.94** vs. 0.66/0.67/0.74.
    - QA: 0.92/0.91/0.98 vs. 0.95/0.95/0.97 (HalluDetect slightly higher on Acc/F1; HalluShift higher on PRAUC).
    - Summarization: 0.99/0.99/0.99 vs. 0.98/0.98/0.99 (tie).
- **How HalluShift / ARREST relate to MIND.**
  - HalluShift is conceptually a *generalisation* of MIND: instead of using one layer's hidden state, it tracks distribution shifts across all layers and fuses with token-probability features.
  - HalluShift drops the layer-selection problem (which MIND solves heuristically by picking last layer); MIND is therefore a *simpler baseline* of the same family.
  - Both use a small MLP, no human labels (HalluShift uses BLEURT-derived labels as in HaloScope), and report on the same QA dataset suite.
  - ARREST is *orthogonal* to the student's MIND work — it is a mitigation method that builds on detection, not a detection method itself.
- **Relevance to the student's project.** **Extremely high.** This is the most natural "prior thesis at the same lab" reference and provides the exact dataset suite and evaluation protocol the student plans to use. Specifically:
  - The student's multi-task evaluation list (TruthfulQA, TriviaQA, CoQA, TydiQA-GP, HaluEval-{QA,Summ,Dialog}) is the **exact** Dasgupta dataset suite. The student is essentially asking: "How well does a MIND baseline do on Dasgupta's evaluation, before adding HalluShift-style multi-layer + probabilistic features?"
  - The chapter should be transparent that HalluShift represents the state-of-the-art *internal-state* baseline as of mid-2025 and that MIND is the simpler ablation it generalises.

## Key open problems the surveys / papers identify

1. **No standard hallucination detection benchmark across tasks.** Ji et al. 2024 note that hallucination is defined task-by-task (intrinsic vs. extrinsic shifts), and benchmarks like WikiBio-GPT3 (passage-level Wikipedia bios), HaluEval (per-task synthetic hallucinations), TruthfulQA (multi-choice / generation), AVeriTeC (real-world claims) measure different things. The student's multi-task evaluation directly addresses this gap.
2. **Generalisation across LLMs.** MIND's Table 4 shows that hallucination classifiers do *not* transfer well across LLMs — each LLM needs its own training data. HaloScope likewise reports better in-distribution transfer than cross-distribution. The student should report per-LLM training rather than a single classifier.
3. **Layer selection is unsolved.** SAPLMA picks the 20th layer for OPT-6.7B and middle for LLaMA-2-7B; HaloScope finds 8–14 best for LLaMA-2-7B; MIND uses last layer; HalluShift automates layer selection. The student's chapter should discuss this as an open problem.
4. **Computational efficiency vs. accuracy.** SelfCheckGPT-Prompt is best but 1800% of generation time. MIND is ~3% of generation time. The student's chapter should frame "real-time" as the practical desideratum for production hallucination detection.
5. **Black-box vs. white-box assumptions.** Closed APIs (GPT-4 etc.) preclude hidden-state methods. The student should note that MIND-style methods apply only when internals are accessible (open-weights models).
6. **Atomic / span-level hallucination.** Most methods detect at sentence or passage level; sentences often contain *both* factual and hallucinated atoms (FActScore, Min et al. 2023). The student's MIND implementation operates at entity-substitution granularity, which is one step finer than sentence-level.
7. **Snowballing / cascading hallucinations.** A hallucinated early token corrupts subsequent generation (Azaria & Mitchell motivate this with the Pluto example). MIND's per-token probability output is well-suited to detect early.
8. **Calibration of "I know".** Kadavath et al. show that P(IK) is well-calibrated in-distribution but breaks under distribution shift. Detection methods should be evaluated as classifiers *and* as calibrated probabilistic estimators (Brier score, ECE), not just AUC.
9. **Lexical metrics encourage hallucination.** The lexical-notes file argues that benchmarks scored by BLEU/ROUGE reward fluent paraphrase even of hallucinated content; semantic / probing metrics are needed.

## What Sharanya Dasgupta's thesis covers and how it relates to the student's work

Dasgupta's thesis spans three chapters of contribution: (i) HalluShift detection, (ii) ARREST mitigation/safety, and (iii) full empirical evaluation. The detection chapter is the directly comparable work. Concretely:

- **Same eval suite.** The student should match TruthfulQA, TriviaQA, CoQA, TydiQA-GP, HaluEval-{QA,Summ,Dialog}. The MIND chapter is essentially an ablation of HalluShift that drops (a) multi-layer feature fusion and (b) the probabilistic feature stack, retaining only the hidden-state probe with an entity-substitution self-supervised label.
- **Same hardware.** Dasgupta used a single RTX 3090 (24 GB). The student's Llama-3.2-1B 4-bit setup is even smaller and Colab/Kaggle-friendly.
- **Same setup conventions** (Section 6.1.2 of Dasgupta): BLEURT for ground-truth similarity scoring; max output length 64 tokens; greedy decoding. The student should adopt these conventions for direct comparability.
- **Same baselines.** Dasgupta reports Perplexity, LN-entropy, Semantic Entropy, Lexical Similarity, EigenScore, SelfCheckGPT, Verbalize, Self-evaluation, CCS, CCS*, HaloScope. The student's chapter should at minimum report SelfCheckGPT-NLI, HaloScope, and HalluShift as comparison points.
- **Where the student goes further.** The student is implementing MIND specifically on **Llama-3.2-1B 4-bit-quantised**, which neither MIND (LLaMA-2-13B), HaloScope (LLaMA-2-7B / OPT-6.7B), nor HalluShift (LLaMA-2-7B / 3.1-8B / OPT-6.7B / Vicuna / Qwen) reports. Adding the 1B-scale data point to the MIND family is a small but novel contribution. Dasgupta does not include 4-bit / smaller models.
- **Cross-task pseudo-labelling caveat.** MIND's labelling depends on Wikipedia continuation matching. For QA datasets (TruthfulQA, TriviaQA, etc.) the student must adapt the pseudo-label generator — or train the MIND probe on Wikipedia continuations and *only test* on the QA datasets (e.g. evaluate hallucination of LLM-generated answers vs. gold answers by BLEURT, as Dasgupta does). The chapter should be explicit about which protocol is used.

## Suggested chapter structure (for the dissertation)

1. **Introduction & problem definition** (use Ji et al. taxonomy: intrinsic vs. extrinsic; factuality vs. faithfulness).
2. **Why lexical metrics fail** (the lexical-notes file motivates this).
3. **Family 1 — Self-evaluation / calibration** (Kadavath 2022). Frame as "the model is asked to reflect on itself".
4. **Family 2 — Sampling-based / consistency** (SelfCheckGPT, with all 5 variants). Most general black-box baseline.
5. **Family 3 — Internal-state probing** (SAPLMA → MIND → HaloScope → HalluShift). Show the trajectory from hand-crafted-labels to fully-unsupervised to multi-layer / multi-feature.
6. **Family 4 — Retrieval-augmented verification** (FEVER 2024 / AVeriTeC). Position as orthogonal — useful but slow and dependent on external knowledge.
7. **Datasets & evaluation protocols** — the cross-coverage table above.
8. **Open problems** — layer selection, cross-LLM generalisation, span-level granularity, calibration, computational cost.
9. **Positioning the student's contribution** — reimplementing MIND with Llama-3.2-1B 4-bit and extending to the Dasgupta-style multi-task evaluation, providing a small-model ablation of HalluShift's hidden-state component.
